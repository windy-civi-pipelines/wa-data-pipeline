Woodson and Jesse E. Moorland founded the association, Study of Negro Life and History, dedicating the association to research and promoting achievements by Black people in a time when he saw Black history "overlooked, ignored, and even suppressed by the writers of history textbooks and the teachers who use them"; andWHEREAS, The beginnings of Black history in the United States was identified by the unfairly strenuous struggle of slavery, Jim Crow, and segregation that lasted far too long; andWHEREAS, Even today, over 162 years after Black slaves were freed, Black Americans continue to face injustice and unfairness; andWHEREAS, The 2025 Black History Month theme, "African Americans and Labor," brings to light the great deeds and efforts of African Americans to the workforce of the United States; andWHEREAS, Martha Jones became the first Black woman to receive a patent for her improvement to the Corn Husker, Sheller in 1868, just three years after the passage of the 13th Amendment; andWHEREAS, In 1889, William Owen Bush became the first Black American to serve in the Washington Territorial Legislature representing Thurston County; andWHEREAS, Representatives Charles Stokes and Marjorie Pitter King were the first Black American man and woman to serve in the legislature following the proclamation of Washington statehood; andWHEREAS, Shirley Chisholm became the first Black woman elected to Congress in 1968, where she worked on the Education and Labor Committee and helped form the Black Caucus, and she made history again in 1972, when she became the first Black woman of a major party to run for a presidential nomination; andWHEREAS, In the words of poet and State Representative Maurice N. Corbett, who was born to an enslaved mother in 1859, Black Americans had grown accustomed to adversity before gaining freedom, "But worked he with celerity, to leave to his posterity, not laborer's legacy alone, but something real to call his own"; andWHEREAS, Black individuals are woven tight into the fabric of all aspects of our shared life, their work, joy, and love driving this nation forward; andWHEREAS, A society supports what it doesn't work to change. Knowing this, Black activists have worked tirelessly to move our reality closer to an equal world; andWHEREAS, Whatever human virtue can be imagined, so too can a Black individual be found to have represented that good, and so we in the state of Washington resolve to acknowledge that representation. In this month of February in the year 2025, we move with the most sincere of intentions to center our Black sisters and brothers, friends and mothers in our hearts, minds, and conversations;NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED, That the Washington State Senate celebrate Black History Month and recognize the innumerable contributions of Black Americans to the lives of Washingtonians.I, Sarah Bannister, Secretary of the Senate,do hereby certify that this is a true andcorrect copy of Senate Resolution 8617,adopted by the SenateFebruary 20, 2025SARAH BANNISTERSecretary of the Senate ================================================================================ Raw Text: ï»¿SENATE RESOLUTION8617BySenators Lovick, Hasegawa, and MuzzallWHEREAS, Dr. Carter G.
